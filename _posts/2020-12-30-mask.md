_In this post, I explain how I modified the [Miller LPR-100](https://www.google.com/search?q=miller+ML00895) industrial respirator to block off its exhalation valve. The result is a comfortable respirator that I believe offers a high level of protection against airborne diseases to myself and others._

__

The effectiveness of a mask can be broken down into two parts: how well the mask fits on your face, and the filtration efficiency of the mask. A further important consideration is comfort.

Respirators that meet the [NIOSH][niosh] N95 or N99 standards for filtration efficiency, such as the N95 respirators pictured below, are popular in healthcare settings.

<img src="/images/mask/3M_N95_Particulate_Respirator.jfif" width="50%">  
_3M N95 respirator_

In my experience, these have two main downsides:
- They may be scarce during pandemics.
- The tight elastic band that ensures a good fit also makes the respirators very uncomfortable for extended use.

There also exist N95+ masks designed for industrial tasks that produce harmful airborne particles (such as welding or paint spraying). 

<img src="/images/mask/miller_respirator_stock.png" width="50%"><img src="/images/mask/3m-half-facepiece-reusable-respirator.jpg"  width="50%">
_High filtration efficiency respirators for industrial use. [Miller LPR-100](https://www.google.com/search?q=miller+ML00895) (left), 3M 6200 (right)._

Compared to healthcare N95s, these respirators:
- are more widely available
- achieve similar or superior fit by using silicone shaped like a human face
- are much more comfortable, mainly because they:
    - spread the pressure onto a wider area of skin
    - have adjustable straps

Their fatal flaw when it comes to disease control is that (at least for those I have seen) they have an exhalation valve that allows unfiltered air to exit the mask.

# My proposal
I think I've found a way to achieve the best of both worlds: the comfort and availability of industrial masks, and the third-party protection offered by valveless masks.

Let's look at how the valves in industrial masks work. I'll be using the [Miller Electric LPR-100 Half Mask Respirator](https://www.google.com/search?q=miller+ML00895) (for a full explanation of this choice see the section below).

The exhalation valve is at the front. There are also two inhalation valves, one on each side between the mouth and the filter. These only allow air to come into the mask from outside, forcing all the exhaled air to go through the exhalation valve (instead of some of it going back through the filter).

<img src="/images/mask/miller_valves_0.jpg" width="50%"><img src="/images/mask/miller_valves_1.jpg" width="50%">
_Miller  LPR-100 valves_

We need to disable both the inhalation and exhalation valves:
* The unfiltered exhalation valve should be completely sealed off.
* In order to allow the user to exhale, the inhalation valves need to be turned into simple holes that allow two-way air circulation.

## Exhalation valve
We can seal off the exhalation valve from the outside with tape[^altmethods]. On the Miller respirator, there is a little plastic cage covering the valve, this cage can be taped over.

<img src="/images/mask/tape_0.jpg" width="50%"><img src="/images/mask/tape_1.jpg" width="50%">
_Tape on exhalation cage_

I am using [painter's tape](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B00004Z4DU/ref=ppx_yo_dt_b_asin_title_o01_s00?ie=UTF8&psc=1) because it's supposed to pull off without leaving a residue of glue. I don't think it matters much what kind of tape you use.

You can check the seal of your tape by pressing the mask onto your face and attempting to exhale (with the inhalation valves intact). Air should only be able to escape through the sides of the mask.

## Inhalation valves
The inhalation valves are removable and can be pulled out. They are very thin and feel like they might be about to break when you pull them out, but I have been able to pull four of them out without a problem.

<img src="/images/mask/miller_valves_1.jpg" width="50%"><img src="/images/mask/valve_hand.jpg" width="50%">
_Touching a valve (left), a valve after it has been pulled out (right)_


<img src="/images/mask/valves_plugs.jpg" width="50%">

Pushing the valves back in is easy.

The tape can be removed and the valves re-inserted, making my modification is fully reversible.

[^altmethods]: I tried two other methods before I settled on using tape: gluing a thin silicon wafer over the valve on the _inside_ of the mask, and applying glue to the valve directly. Both these methods are entirely inferior and should not be used.

## Also use a surgical mask
Even if you're using the tape technique, I recommend also covering the respirator with a surgical mask, since this has no downsides and might have some benefit. The seal on the exhalation valve might not be perfect and may get worse over time, so an extra layer of filtration, however imperfect, is a good backup. I suspect it's also beneficial because it makes what you're doing legible to others. You don't want to explain this weird tape business to strangers. There's a certain irony in pretending to use a technique that exposes people to more risk in order to avoid negative judgements from those same people... 

# Potential concerns
Messing around with my own modified mask designs might seem unwise. How do I know I haven't messed up something crucial, endagering myself and others?

What are the relvant alternatives to my recommendation? 

My best guess is that constantly wearing a correctly fitted medical N95, with the really tight elastic bands, is very slightly safer for others in expectation than what I recommend (due to risks of things going wrong, like the tape getting unstuck). For me, these respirators are prohibitively uncomfortable, and I have seen few people use them. In reality, I think the realistic alternatives are cloth and surgical masks. I am relatively confident that my technique is an improvement on that, for both the user and third parties.

I assign a low chance that my recommendation is harmful. The probability mass I assign to harm comes mostly from unknown unknowns and the fact that official agencies don't recommend my technique, not from any specific evidence of risks.

I currently don't plant to prioritize writing a full detailed justification of these beliefs for a sceptical audience. Writing such a thing is a great deal of effort and there are benefits to getting the idea out earlier. In order to let you come to the best conclusion for you given your knowledge and risk tolerance, I'm trying to lay out most of the evidence against my proposal that I've considered (without giving the full reasoning for why I find this evidence insufficienly persuasive on balance).

I am not an expert in disease control. I studied economics and philosophy and then worked as a researcher.

## CDC recommendation
To be clear, my proposal goes against the CDC recommendation. The [CDC recommendation](https://blogs.cdc.gov/niosh-science-blog/2020/09/08/source-control/), in a blog post from August 8 2020, is:
> If only a respirator with an exhalation valve is available and source control is needed, cover the exhalation valve with a surgical mask, procedure mask, or a cloth face covering that does not interfere with the respirator fit.

There is no evidence that they considered the seemingly superior tape technique as a possibility when creating this guidance.

If you care a lot about following official guidance, you could use the indutrial respirator + surgical mask technique, which would still be a great improvement over wearing just a surgical or cloth mask.

## Exhaling through filter
Could exhaling through the filter be a bad thing somehow? Though I wasn't able to find any source making an explicit statement on this, I am reasssured by the following:
- healthcare N95s (without valves) are designed to be exhaled through
- this is not mentioned as a potential issue in discussion of tape technique by others (below)

## Discussion of tape technique by others
* [This NIOSH study](https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/docs/2021-107/pdfs/2021-107.pdf?id=10.26616/NIOSHPUB2021107) tested three modifications of valved respirators: covering the valve on the interior with surgical tape, covering the valve on the interior with an electrocardiogram (ECG) pad, and stretching a surgical mask over the exterior of the respirator.
    * They found that "penetration was 23% for the masked-over mitigation; penetration was 5% for the taped mitigation; penetration was 2% for the [ECG pad] mitigation". I would be very interested in more discussion of why the ECG pad did so much better than the surgical tape, the authors don't say much. One guess could be that the ECG pad has a more powerful adhesive, which would suggest that it's important to choose a strongly adhesive tape if implementing my technique.
    * When discussing the choice of modification strategies, the authors wrote that "two concerns are that the adhesive could pull away from the surface, thereby not blocking airflow to the same degree over time, and that these adhesives could contain chemicals that have toxicological effects."

![study](/images/mask/niosh_study.PNG)

* In an [FAQ released by 3M](https://multimedia.3m.com/mws/media/1791526O/respiratory-protection-faq-general-public-tb.pdf), in response to the question of whether one should tape over the exhalation valve, they wrote "3M does not recommend that tape be placed over the exhalation valve", but do not give any reasons for this beyond the fact that it may become "more difficult to breathe through ... if the exhalation valve is taped shut". This is a positive update, since if there was an obvious flaw I would have expected 3M to mention it.
* The state of Maine's Department of Public Safety [recommends against tape-covering](https://www.maine.gov/ems/sites/maine.gov.ems/files/inline-files/2020-08-21%20Operational%20Bulletin%20Regarding%20Masks%20with%20Exhalation%20Valves.pdf), but merely because "this would be considered altering the device and violates the manufacturer’s recommendation".

# Choice of industrial respirator
## Respirator
The two models I have tried are the [3M 6502QL](https://www.google.com/search?q=3M%206502QL) and the [Miller LPR-100](https://www.google.com/search?q=miller+ML00895)[^m]. The Miller is more expensive, and is the choice that I recommend. Since 3M is such a huge player in this market, I think there's a decent chance that the Miller is in fact one of the very best options that exists.

The Miller model comes with replaceable P100-rated filters, while the 3M can be used with many types of filters and cartridges.

## Filters
If you're going to buy the Miller, this section is irrelavant, because the Miller only allows one type of filter.

**The weight of the mask is a crucial determinant of comfort**. I originally used the 3M mask with the 3M 60926 cartridges, which filter gases and vapors as well as particles. This was a mistake, as filtering gases and vapors is irrelavant from the point of view of of infectious disease, and these cartridges are much heavier than the 3M 2097 P100 filters. Switching to the lighter filters made a world of difference, now wearing the 3M doesn't bother me at all.

<img src="/images/mask/3m_weight_cartridge.jpg" width="50%"><img src="/images/mask/3m_weight_filter.jpg" width="50%">  
_The 3M 6502QL respirator weighs 395 g. with 3M 60926 cartridges, but only 128 g. with 3M 2097 filters, a 68% reduction._

You should use filters that are rated N100, R100 or P100. The "100" [means](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NIOSH_air_filtration_rating) that 99.97% of particles smaller than 0.3 micrometres are filtered out. The letters N, R and P refer to whether the filter is still effective when exposed to oil-based aerosols, this should be irrelevant for our purposes.


[niosh]: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NIOSH_air_filtration_rating
